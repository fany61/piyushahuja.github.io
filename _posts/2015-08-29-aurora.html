In the summer of 2016, I interned with Aurora Energy Research, an energy analytics provider with a strong presence in the UK. When I joined their modelling division, they were preparing to enter the German market. This presented a very exciting learning opportunity - the company still had a startup like work environment. The team responsible for the German launch had only three other people - so there was a lot of work to be done, a lot of impact to be made. I was given a lot of responsbility and full ownership of my work.

The German launch presentation was to be attended by many important leaders of the German energy sector. An important ingredient of the presentation was our analysis and and forecast of the cost of Germany’s renewable energy subsidy policy (EEG) for the next twenty years. I was tasked with preparing this forecast - termed the (Renewable Energy Sources) Support model.

We divided the model into different subproblems: (1) forecasting capacities for different renewable energy sources in Germany, and (2) calculating subsidies that flowed into all plants based on the forecasted capcities, (3) calculating the burden of subsidies on German consumers, which is levied as EEG surcharge by the German government.1

This proved to be a real challenge. The German energy policy (EEG) had been amended every year since 2000, and different policy versions applied to different energy power plants depending on when they were commissioned. To create a model with sufficient accuracy we needed extensive data on power plant type, their year of commission, which plants had opted for which marketing plan, estimates on refurbishment, life cycle and expiry - data which wasn’t always easily available. My unfamiliarity with German language didn’t make the task any easier. It would have certainly helped - be it reading policy documents written in German, understand the reports of native German thinktanks, or simply interpreting data labelled with German keywords.

But I was determined, and I persisted. I read all the policy documents. I used data from whatever publicly available datasets I could lay my hands on. And I benchmarked the capacity forecasts from reports by other thinktanks. In a little over a month, I the model was complete. It that forecasted renewable electricity surcharge on German consumers for the next 20 years. The excel model was flexible enough to incorporate changes in Aurora’s outlook for the German energy market and future energy policy.

It was a tremendous learning experience where I learnt making a dashboard on excel and using data to measure and predict policy effects. I also developed a know-how of renewable energy technologies. With the RES support model, the company was able to prepare intelligent, data driven outlook on the eeg surcharge, energy retail price and the renewable energy capacity and generation in general for the crucial German launch presentation in November.

As a separate initiative, I optimized the data cleaning and loading process used by the team, speeding up the process­ by. 7200%. I accomplished this by rewriting the previous clunky code in an elegant, dynamic and efficient script. I learnt computer languages R and basic Python for this, which would be useful for data manipulation, analysis and visualization tasks that might be required at Oxera.

As a third task, I also conducted a regression analysis investigating whether the annual average load factor of a county’s offshore wind energy varies as it builds more capacity. Regression would be useful in any cause-and-effect economic analysis at Oxera.

The Model:

The optimal output to meet the system load from a number of electricity generation facilities in the short term is determined by a model known as economic dispatch. The main idea is that to serve the load at the minimum cost, the set of generators with the lowest marginal cost must be used first, with the marginal cost of the final generator needed to meet the load setting the system marginal cost. This is the cost of delivering one MW of additional electricity into the system (under given transmission and operational constraints).

The power generation from different renewable technologies is a result of many contributing factors. How much installed capacity there is depends on economics and government policy (e.g solar and wind power our encouraged via high subsidy rates and priority grid feed in, but coal is discouraged by levying carbon emission costs, biomass, since its expensive, is being clamped down on, nuclear power is being shut down, and so on.). The load factor (i.e. generation/capacity), that is power generation once capacity is taken into account, then depends on technology related factors (the generation from solar and wind are variable and governed by natural factors: solar and wind offshore produce more during the day, wind onshore produces more during the night, and then there are seasonal variations).

As an modelling team intern at Aurora Energy Research, I was working with a model whose output was in the form of hundreds of excel sheets with huge amounts of data. This data was cleaned (R), manipulated (Java) and then fed in the database (SQL) using a programming script which was inelegant, error prone, and inefficient. For example, there was a separate code segment for each file, even though most functions were common across files. For every new excel file one needed to add a new code segment. Also, the program employed context switching between Java, R and SQL engines for every row of data, which massively increased the overheads. I volunteered to work on rewriting the script in my spare time. I myself wasn’t proficient in the programming languages used, but my algorithmic background helped me understand the code and spot potential improvements. I taught myself R and SQL syntax online, and changed the layout of the script. Instead of a separate static code segment for each file, I designed a generic wrapper function, which would work dynamically with any file input. I also implemented an algorithm in which the rows were inserted in bulk, which reduced the context switching costs by an order of magnitude. The new script was faster, elegant and efficient. Importantly, the data loading time improved from 12 hours to just 10 mins. In the end, not only did I pick up valuable skills, but I also got to create long-lasting positive externalities for the entire team.

PROBLEM SOLVING Context: The years 2008-2015 saw rapid expansion in offshore wind energy for many countries; for example, UK’s offshore wind capacity grew from just 88 MW in 2008 to 3 GW by 2013. By 2015, Denmark’s Offshore Wind Capacity totalled 1.2 GW, Belgium’s around 700 MW and Germany’s was 1.5 GW+. Stronger wind speeds are available offshore compared to on land, so offshore wind power’s contribution in terms of electricity supplied is higher, and unlike wind over the continent, offshore breezes can be strong in the afternoon, matching the time when people are using the most electricity. As such, as countries make a transition from fossil fuels to renewables for their primary energy needs, offshore wind power forms an intergral part of their energy policies. Germany, for example, plans to increase its offshore wind capacity to 6.5 GW by 2020 and 15 GW by 2030.

Unlike conventional power plants, the net output or electricity generated from wind farms is uncertain. Depending on the variation on wind speed across time, it can be quite volatile. However, as a country installs more and more offshore wind power, we expect that the volatility (measured by standard deviation) in its netoutput from those to decrease. This is because the output of one wind farm is not always positively correlated with the output of another wind farm. (As the simplest case, imagine two wind farms. If at one point of time one is experiencing high wind and the other low wind, and vice versa at another point of time, then their net output remains almost uniform, even though they experience high degrees of variation in output individually)

T: In this study, we put our hypothesis that volatility in net offshore output decreases with increasing total capacity to test. To account for variation in electriciy generation due to installation or decommissioning of further capacities, we track the change in standard deviation of hourly load factor* (instead of net output) with total installed capacity. We find that decreasing logarithmic curves allow a good fit for UK, DEU and DNK capacity vs. LF stddev observeations. The curves provide us with a reliable estimate for the future volatility in offshore output as countries expand their capacities. Load factor (or Capacity Factor) is a measure of how much electricity a power plant actually produces compared to how much it would produce if it operated at full capacity at that point of time (Example: Take a five-megawatt wind turbine. If it produces power of two megawatts, then its load factor at that time instant is 40% (2÷5 = 0.40, i.e. 40%).

A:

R: On the scatterplot, we find the pattern (trendline) of standard deviation of LF vs Capacities for UK is quite apart from those of other countries, while the trendline esp. for Germany Denmark, but also Belgium, are closely bunched together. UK experience lesser std dev (lesser variation) in wind output than other countries for the same total installed capacity. This could be because UK is a big island and has a larger range of locations for wind farms, while Germany, Denmark and Belgium have all their offshore windfarms spread over a small location. (In other words, UK has a more diversified portfolio of wind farms). We find that decreasing logarithmic curves allow a good fit for UK, DEU and DNK capacity vs. LF stddev observeations. As countries grow more offshore wind farms, the variation in offshore output would continue decreasing (as wind output becomes more uniform due to a larger spread of windfarms) until it becomes near-flat (when further decrease in variance by installing more farms becomes very difficult - almost impossible)
